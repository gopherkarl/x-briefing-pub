Epistemological Perspective

Popper and Deutsch on Economics

An epistemological critique of forecasting, wealth, central planning, and the market as a mechanism for error-correction.

Author Karl
Co-Author Shubham Mehrish
Published July 06, 2026
Topic Epistemology & Wealth

Introduction

The epistemological frameworks of Karl Popper and David Deutsch offer a profound, counter-intuitive, and highly critical perspective on economics. Rather than treating economics as a system of numbers, equations, or resources, both view it through the lens of epistemology: the creation, testing, and correction of knowledge.

To understand this view, we can break it down into four core principles: the impossibility of macroeconomic forecasting, the market as a mechanism for error-correction, the epistemic definition of wealth, and the rejection of centralized planning in favor of "piecemeal engineering."

1. The Impossibility of Economic Forecasting (The Poverty of Historicism)

The most direct Popperian critique of macroeconomics lies in his classic treatise, The Poverty of Historicism. Popper's argument is a logical proof that destroys the foundation of long-term macroeconomic forecasting and historical prediction:

  • The course of human history is strongly influenced by the growth of human knowledge.
  • We cannot predict, by rational or scientific methods, the future growth of our scientific knowledge (if we could, we would already possess that knowledge today).
  • Therefore, we cannot predict the future course of human history or the economy.

"If there is such a thing as growing human knowledge, then we cannot anticipate today what we shall know only tomorrow."

— Karl Popper, The Poverty of Historicism

Macroeconomists often try to build highly complex, mathematical models to predict GDP growth, inflation, or the long-term impact of technologies. Popperians view these predictive models as a form of scientism or historicism—the mistaken belief that there are historical "laws" or trends that can be discovered and projected forward.

Because the economy is driven by ideas, choices, and technologies that have not yet been invented, macroeconomic forecasting is fundamentally impossible. Any model that pretends to predict the economic future is denying the open-ended nature of human creativity.

2. Wealth as "The Repertoire of Physical Preparation to Solve Problems"

In The Beginning of Infinity, David Deutsch redefines the very concept of wealth. In classical economics, wealth is often defined in terms of physical assets, capital, or GDP. Deutsch strips away this materialist view and defines wealth in purely epistemic terms:

"Wealth is the repertoire of physical transformations that one is capable of causing."

— David Deutsch, The Beginning of Infinity

Under this view, a society is not wealthy because it has a lot of oil, gold, or factories. It is wealthy because it possesses the knowledge of how to transform those physical resources to solve human problems.

  • The Epistemic Ratchet: Resources themselves are not finite in any ultimate sense; our knowledge of how to use them is what is limited. For example, uranium was useless dirt until we discovered the knowledge of nuclear physics; silicon was common sand until we discovered the knowledge of semiconductors.
  • Optimism: All economic limits, resource crises, and Malthusian traps are not absolute boundaries of nature, but merely problems arising from a lack of knowledge. Because "problems are solvable" and "problems are inevitable," economic progress is open-ended.

3. The Market as an Evolutionary System of Conjectures & Refutations

Popper’s core scientific methodology is Conjectures and Refutations—we progress by making bold guesses (creating "Good Explanations" in Deutsch's view) and then ruthlessly trying to find errors in them. The Popperian view of the market is that it is simply evolutionary epistemology in action:

  • Conjectures: Every new startup, product, investment, or business model is a conjecture—a theory about what consumers want, how to produce something more efficiently, or how to solve a specific problem.
  • Refutations / Criticism: The market acts as the criticism engine. Bankruptcy, losses, and consumer rejection are the "refutations" of bad economic theories. Profits and growth are temporary corroborations.
  • Coercion vs. Creativity: Just as dogmatism and censorship halt scientific progress by preventing error correction, government interventions (such as subsidies, bailouts, and regulatory barriers) halt economic progress by preventing the refutation of bad ideas. When the state bails out a failing company, it is keeping a refuted theory alive on life support, which prevents capital and human minds from being redeployed to better, unrefuted conjectures.

4. "Piecemeal Engineering" vs. "Utopian Social Planning"

In The Open Society and Its Enemies, Popper draws a sharp distinction between two approaches to social and economic change:

Feature Utopian Social Engineering (Central Planning) Piecemeal Social Engineering (The Open Market)
Assumption The "planners" possess the central knowledge to redesign society or the economy from scratch. No single participant or agency can possess the highly decentralized knowledge scattered throughout society. And neither can they predict the growth of such knowledge.
Scale Large-scale, systemic, and top-down (e.g., massive state-run industrial policies, price controls, socialist five-year plans). Small-scale, localized, and bottom-up (e.g., decentralized enterprise, local trials).
Error-Correction Rigid and resistant to criticism. Planners hide errors because admitting defeat threatens the entire system. Highly error-correcting. Bad investments fail quickly, and policies are monitored and adjusted incrementally.
Stance on Failure Suppresses feedback and forces compliance to make the model "work." Views failures as essential data points for refining theories.

Popper and Deutsch argue that because we are fallible and the economic system is incredibly complex, we must adopt epistemic humility. We cannot know the unintended consequences of our economic policies beforehand. Therefore, technological and economic progress happens when people are free to try ideas, fail, and iterate — markets, engineering culture, and open criticism create feedback loops that continuously improve designs, which Deutsch calls error-correcting institutions. He puts markets in the same category as science and democracy — good explanations only flourish under a rare set of cultural memes that allow for error-correction: freedom of speech, democracy, market economics, and separating politics from science.

In conclusion, the market's economic value and its epistemic value are the same thing: it's a mechanism for cheaply discovering which ideas are wrong and for incentivizing unbounded progress.
