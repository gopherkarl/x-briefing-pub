Engineering Sandeep Bot

A 48-hour sprint in bilingual style mimicry, agentic orchestration, and Popperian error correction.

Author Karl
Co-Author Shubham Mehrish
Published July 12, 2026
Target System Sandeep Bot V2
Explore Architecture Read Epistemology Matrix

Abstract

"This paper documents the 48-hour rapid development and deployment of 'Sandeep Bot,' an automated conversational agent engineered to replicate the highly nuanced, bilingual (Hinglish/Urdu) comedic style of a specific WhatsApp group participant. We explore the architectural progression from local parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) on Apple Silicon to an agile, agentic 'thin-client' system powered by frontier models. We contrast the limitations of small-scale open-weights model fine-tuning against structured context engineering and tool-driven learning, framing the entire engineering loop through Karl Popper’s critical rationalism. Finally, we detail the real-world constraints, diagnostic protocols, and security roadblocks of integrating automated agents into the sandboxed WhatsApp ecosystem, culminating in the detection and mitigation of a silent session restriction (shadow-ban)."

1. Introduction: The Bilingual Pun Hypothesis

Humor is a sophisticated expression of human intelligence. It is not merely a collection of statistical token associations but a complex knowledge structure that bridges language, culture, and shared social history. Replicating an individual's humor programmatically represents a formidable challenge; replicating a bilingual pun-writing style introduces an exponential level of complexity.

The Linguistic and Computational Challenge

"Sandeep Bot" was conceived to automate witty, dry, sarcastic Hinglish and Urdu wordplay within a close-knit group chat named BITWA. The target persona employs a conversational style characterized by:

1. Phonetic Drift: Seamlessly mapping English words onto phonetic near-collisions in Hindi or Urdu (e.g., transforming "dry bar" into the Hindi phrase baar baar [again and again], or playing on the resort brand Azul to mock fuzool kharch [wasteful expenditure]).

2. Name & Initial Distortion: Transforming group members' names or initials into comedic portmanteaus (e.g., mapping "Krishna NV" to Envy, or "Amit Khetarpal" to Cater-pal).

3. High-Contrast Parody: Treating mundane personal updates with the verbose gravity of corporate press releases or academic definitions.

4. Lyrical Overlays: Parodying Bollywood songs, Urdu shayari, or Kabir couplets to describe modern group events.

Standard monolingual language models struggle with this style. They suffer from strong English-centric bias, lack the cultural context of South Asian code-switching, and fail to grasp the high-signal, low-context inside jokes of a closed social circle.

2. Model Evaluation & Local Architecture

To achieve zero-cost, private, and high-performance execution, we restricted our initial deployment to a local Apple M5 Pro Mac (48 GB Unified Memory). We evaluated two open-weights families for the classification and style generation phases: Meta's Llama-3-8B-Instruct and Google's Gemma (specifically Gemma 2/4 variants).

Metric / Feature Llama-3-8B-Instruct Gemma (12B Class)
Local Framework MLX (Apple Silicon Native) Ollama
Quantization 4-bit (Safetensors) 4-bit / 8-bit (GGUF)
Native Multilingual Prior High English, Moderate Hindi Exceptional Multilingual Tokenizer
Context Window 8K tokens 8K - 32K tokens
Reasoning Class Structured, direct Deep hierarchical, better formatting
VRAM Footprint ~5.5 GB (Highly efficient) ~8.5 GB

Structural Decision: We selected Gemma (via Ollama) as our primary data-classification engine. The Gemma tokenizer proved significantly more robust at handling raw phonetic Hinglish and transliterated Urdu scripts without splitting them into meaningless sub-word fragments. For the fine-tuning target, we selected Llama-3-8B-Instruct due to the mature support for native GPU parameter-efficient fine-tuning under the Apple Silicon-focused MLX framework.

3. Data Pipeline Hurdles & Synthetic Iteration

The foundational constraint of any machine learning project is data quality. Our raw material consisted of over 24,000 WhatsApp chats spanning several years.

The Gritty Reality of the Data Phase

We extracted 281 candidate puns from the chat logs. However, simple token frequency analysis revealed that the raw dataset was highly contaminated. Over 60% of the entries were purely English wordplay or standard, non-stylistic sarcasm. We used a local Gemma model running a custom script (classify_puns.py) to programmatically label and partition the dataset into five stylistic categories:

Bilingual Hinglish/Urdu 32.0%
Name-based Wordplay 17.1%
Pop Culture / Songs 8.2%
Portmanteau 1.4%
Pure English / Sarcasm 41.3%

To enforce bilingual style compliance, we executed a hard filter, stripping out the 166 purely English sarcasm records. This left us with over 100 highly concentrated Hinglish/Urdu entries to prevent the base model's English priors from diluting the target style.

The General Mills Outlier

During the curation phase, we encountered a massive structural anomaly: the "General Mills" press release. When a group member accepted a corporate role, Sandeep wrote a verbose, multi-paragraph mock corporate press release detailing the immediate collapse of General Mills' stock value. This record introduced a severe outlier in terms of sequence length (over 1,200 tokens) compared to the standard, single-sentence group chat puns (average of 15–30 tokens).

We made the engineering decision to isolate this mock press release from the fine-tuning split and treat it as a distinct "stylistic format" to be injected via zero-shot prompts rather than weight adaptation, ensuring optimal padding sizes during model batching.

Synthetic Data Expansion

With just a small number of natural bilingual puns, our dataset was dangerously sparse. We pioneered a synthetic data generation loop by prompting Gemini 3.5 Flash to act as a cultural validator. We fed the model our seed puns and instructed it to generate culturally congruent, phonetically sound bilingual puns based on hypothetical group chat context. This expanded our training partition to 97 training samples and 18 validation samples, preserving a clean split.

4. The Parameter-Fine-Tuning Loop & Its Limits

Using the MLX local virtual environment, we initialized Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) fine-tuning on the Llama-3-8B base model.

The Attention-Only Refutation (Run 1)

Our first training run used a standard LoRA configuration targeting only the model's attention projections (q_proj and v_proj).

lora_config.yaml - Attention Only
# Run 1: Attention-Only Config
model: "/Users/karl/whatsapp_puns/Llama-3-8B-Instruct"
train: true
num_layers: 32
adapter_path: "adapters"
learning_rate: 1e-5
epochs: 50
lora_parameters:
  keys: ["q_proj", "v_proj"]
  rank: 8
  alpha: 16

The model converged to a training loss of 0.052, suggesting complete style absorption. However, empirical testing refuted this assumption. When challenged with fresh chat context, the model generated grammatically perfect English sarcasm but completely failed to output bilingual Hinglish code-switching or Urdu wordplay.

Architectural Insight: Attention-only LoRA adapts conversational "turn-taking" structures and stylistic pacing, but fails to modify the model's vocabulary and lexical bindings. To force a deep bilingual shift, we had to target the Feed-Forward Network (FFN) layers, which govern word-association mappings.

FFN/MLP Layer Targeting (Run 2)

We updated our configuration to target the FFN projections: gate_proj, up_proj, and down_proj. We also doubled the LoRA rank and raised the learning rate.

lora_config.yaml - Full MLP Target
# Run 2: MLP Targeted Config
model: "/Users/karl/whatsapp_puns/Llama-3-8B-Instruct"
train: true
num_layers: 32
adapter_path: "adapters"
learning_rate: 2e-5
epochs: 150
lora_parameters:
  keys: ["q_proj", "v_proj", "k_proj", "o_proj", "gate_proj", "up_proj", "down_proj"]
  rank: 16
  alpha: 32

Validation Loss Curve

1.40 1.20 1.00 0.80
0 50 (Global Min) 100 (Rolled Checkpoint) 150 (Overfit)

As illustrated, the validation loss reached its global minimum at Iteration 50 (0.987) and drifted upward by Iteration 100 (1.210) and Iteration 150 (1.266). This rising loss represented standard over-fitting. To preserve the model's capacity to generalize across diverse group conversations, we terminated the training run at Iteration 150 using SIGTERM and rolled back to the Iteration 100 checkpoint. We fused these weights back into the 4-bit base model.

5. The Agentic Pivot: Building Plan B via Hermes Agent

The aforementioned empirical limitations of the local 8B model led to an architectural pivot. During evaluation we observed that the zero-shot bilingual "conjectures" generated by Gemini 3.5 Flash during our data validation phase (such as mapping "Mazda" to "Mazaa") were vastly superior in comedic timing and linguistic elegance to the weights adapted by local fine-tuning.

The Paradigm Shift: From Weight Modification to Context Adaptability

Shifting from Weights to Context

Instead of forcing a small model to memorize Sandeep's style in its weights, we decided to leverage the massive world-model capabilities of a frontier model. We utilized the Hermes Agent Framework to programmatically capture, structure, and store Sandeep's pun-generation style as an autonomous, dynamic skill. We executed the Hermes /learn command, feeding the agent the raw 281-pun dataset, the stylistic categories, and the conceptual rules of "phonetic drift." This created a persistent, reusable skill: sandeep-pun-writing.

sandeep-pun-writing/SKILL.md
# ~/.hermes/skills/creative/sandeep-pun-writing/SKILL.md
---
name: sandeep-pun-writing
description: Generate high-quality phonetic bilingual puns in the style of Sandeep Dadlani
category: creative
---

## Style Pillars
1. Bilingual Phonetic Drift (English <-> Hinglish/Urdu)
2. Name & Initial Distortion (Krishna NV <-> Envy)
3. High-Contrast Stylistic Parody
4. Bollywood / Folk-Lyrical Overlays

## Step-by-Step Pun Generation Process
1. Isolate the Context Entities (names, brands, places).
2. Scan keywords for sound-alikes in Hindi/Urdu.
3. Target the tease at the group participants' known quirks.
4. Format with sarcasm and trailing emoticons (😜, 😂, 😭, 😃).

Output Constraints & Hyperparameter Tuning

A major challenge of utilizing a highly conversational frontier model like Gemini 3.5 Flash is controlling its default tendency towards verbosity and explanatory introductions (e.g., "Here is a pun for you:"). To enforce Sandeep's characteristically dry and punchy comedic timing, we engineered a twin constraint system of hyperparameter tuning and explicit context boundaries.

First, we locked the model's generation to its highest probability output by setting the sampling temperature to a deterministic minimum (temperature = 0.0 or 0.1), effectively executing a greedy search over the token space to yield the most mathematically cohesive bilingual phonetic alignment. Second, we strictly limited the response to sentence length by embedding rigid length instructions directly into the system template ("Respond in exactly one sentence", "Keep the output under 15 words") and applying a defensive max_tokens barrier of 60 inside the inference script. This dual lock guaranteed that Sandeep Bot's live outputs were restricted to immediate, high-probability punchlines without any corporate preamble or conversational fluff.

6. Epistemological Framework: Popperian Engineering

Engineering is often taught as an inductive process: gather data, train a model, and arrive at a solution. This project strongly refuted that view. Our progress was driven entirely by critical rationalism—the Popperian cycle of making bold conjectures, rigid implementations, immediate refutations, and resulting superior conjectures.

Phase 1: Dataset
Isolating Style from Token Statistics
Conjecture
Bilingual style can be captured by statistics of raw chat files.
Refutation
Raw log was contaminated (41.3%) by English sarcasm, diluting the unique Hinglish priors.
Correction
Rigidly filtered and isolated clean Hinglish/Urdu records to prevent English bias.
Phase 2: Fine-Tuning Layers
Attention Projection Limitations
Conjecture
Adapting standard Q and V projections is sufficient for full style mimicry.
Refutation
Attention Run 1 generated grammatically perfect English sarcasm, but zero language switching.
Correction
FFN (MLP) layers govern lexical associations. Targeted gate, up, and down projections to enable language switching.
Phase 3: Cognitive Capacity
Weight-tuning vs Context World Models
Conjecture
Local 8B model weights can learn complex, multi-layered bilingual phonetic drift.
Refutation
Fine-tuned 8B produced rigid, occasionally hallucinated words; struggled with sophisticated comedic timing.
Correction
Frontier world models have rich multilingual priors. Switched to "thin-client" skill-driven prompting using the Hermes Agent Framework.

7. The Deployment Bottleneck: WhatsApp Ecosystem Realities

Moving from a theoretical model to a live system forced us to confront the highly restrictive, sandboxed architecture of the modern messaging ecosystem.

The Headless Sandbox

The WhatsApp Business API is prohibitively expensive and strictly regulated, banning automated conversational bots on personal phone numbers. To deploy Sandeep Bot into the BITWA group, we had to engineer a "headless" client using whatsapp-web.js (a Node.js wrapper around Puppeteer). This approach introduced unique system bottlenecks:

1. Session Persistence: WhatsApp regularly terminates headless browser sessions. We bypassed this by mounting a persistent User Data Directory (/Users/karl/whatsapp_puns/session/session) inside Chromium, preserving cookies and encryption keys across process crashes.

2. Infinite Self-Reply Loops: In group chats, if a bot reads its own output, it can trigger itself recursively, spamming the group and leading to an immediate phone number ban. We implemented a strict normalization check to ensure the bot ignores its own messages.

3. Rate Limiting: To mimic human typing and avoid trigger-happy automated detection patterns, we routed all outbound messages through an artificial delay queue, simulating a 120 character-per-minute typing speed.

The Silent Shadow-Ban Roadblock

Despite these safeguards, the live bot client eventually hit the ultimate deployment roadblock: Automated Shadow-Banning.

The symptom of this restriction was highly deceptive. The headless Puppeteer session authenticated successfully, joined the BITWA group, and compiled incoming messages without a single exception or console warning. The node script logged that the client was connected, and the bot even computed the appropriate Hinglish puns in real time. However, none of the generated responses appeared on the users' physical phones.

Diagnostic Protocol

To debug this silent failure, we constructed a target validation script (check_msg_delivery.js & send_direct_test.js) to inspect the raw network Acknowledgment (ACK) packets transmitted between our headless client and WhatsApp's websocket servers:

check_msg_delivery.js
// From get_page_logs.js & check_msg_delivery.js diagnostics
client.on('message_create', async msg => {
    console.log(`Message ACK Status: ${msg.ack}`);
    // 0 = ACK_ERROR (Dropped/Rejected by Server)
    // 1 = ACK_PENDING (Locally Queued)
    // 2 = ACK_SERVER (Received by WhatsApp Cloud)
    // 3 = ACK_DEVICE (Successfully delivered to recipient's hand)
});

Running the diagnostic revealed a stark behavioral dichotomy:

• Incoming Messages: Real user messages (such as Sandeep's real-time comments) returned Ack: 3 (ACK_DEVICE), meaning the headless browser successfully received them over the wire.

• Outbound Bot Messages: Every single message sent by the bot's programmatic profile was instantly labeled Ack: 0 (ACK_ERROR) by the server.

This proved that WhatsApp's automated anti-bot heuristic had silently restricted (shadow-banned) our session. The web client was allowed to read the network stream, but the websocket gateway silently dropped all outgoing payloads at the border, completely bypassing standard client-side error-throwing.

Resolution & Recovery

Our Popperian criticism identified that session metadata, once flagged, cannot be rescued. The only path forward was an immediate session reset:

1. Terminate the active Node.js processes and background daemons.

2. Force-delete the Chromium file lock to prevent process collision:
rm -f /Users/karl/whatsapp_puns/session/session/SingletonLock

3. Spin up a fresh Puppeteer browser, force the regeneration of a brand-new QR code, and re-link the physical device under a new clean session.

4. Establish tighter typing-delay curves and random message-read pauses to delay future heuristic flagging.

8. Conclusion: The Modern State of AI

Our 48-hour sprint to build Sandeep Bot serves as a microcosm of the macro-shifts currently occurring across the global field of Artificial Intelligence.

For the past five years, the dominant engineering paradigm was "Data-Heavy Fine-Tuning." The industry assumed that to capture niche behaviors, we had to collect massive datasets, rent expensive GPU clusters, and bake custom weights.

This project demonstrated the limits of that approach. A local 8B model, even with MLP-targeted fine-tuning, lacks the cognitive "elasticity" to generate creative, bilingual phonetic wordplay. It can mimic the structure of a joke but struggles to create the insight behind it.

Conversely, the emerging paradigm of "Thin-Client Agentic Skill Creation" proves highly superior. By combining a high-capacity frontier world model (such as Gemini 3.5) with structured, tool-driven frameworks like Hermes Agent, we can create incredibly sophisticated, highly specific ideas in minutes.

Through the Hermes Agent /learn framework, we do not modify weights; we construct executable knowledge layers that sit on top of universal models. This represents the beginning of infinity for software engineering—a future where software is not compiled, but taught.
